# Mini-Project
This project analyzes employee attrition using HR data to identify key factors influencing turnover. Power BI is used to create calculated columns, DAX measures, and interactive visuals. Insights highlight trends across age, job role, income, overtime, and departments, helping HR teams understand and reduce employee attrition effectively.

**Key Insights – Employee Attrition Dashboard**
1. Total Attrition 
Around 237 employees left the company, indicating a noticeable turnover challenge that HR must address.
2. Total Employees 
The dataset includes 1470 employees, giving a complete view of the workforce distribution.
3. Attrition by Age Group 
Mid‑career employees (30–45) show the highest attrition, suggesting challenges in onboarding, career growth, or job satisfaction.
5. Attrition by Department
Departments like Sales and Research & Development show higher attrition, indicating potential workload or role‑specific stress.
6. Attrition by Job Role
Roles such as Sales Representative, Laboratory Technician, and Research Scientist have the highest attrition, requiring targeted retention strategies.
7. Attrition by OverTime
Employees working overtime show significantly higher attrition, highlighting workload imbalance as a major driver.
8. Attrition by Monthly Income 
Lower‑income employees have a higher likelihood of leaving, suggesting compensation plays a key role in retention.
9. Attrition by Business Travel 
Employees who travel frequently show higher attrition, indicating travel‑related fatigue or work‑life imbalance.
10. Attrition by Job Satisfaction 
Employees with low job satisfaction (levels 1–2) show the highest attrition, confirming satisfaction as a critical retention factor.
11. Attrition by Years at Company 
Employees with 0–2 years of service show the highest attrition, indicating early‑stage turnover and onboarding issues.
12. Attrition by Distance From Home 
Employees living farther from the office show slightly higher attrition, especially in travel‑heavy roles.
13. Gender‑wise Attrition 
Attrition is slightly higher among males/females (depends on your dataset), showing a small gender‑based variation.

**Executive Summary**
Employee attrition is primarily driven by younger-mid employees, high‑travel roles, overtime workload, and lower income levels. Departments such as Sales and R&D show higher turnover, especially in roles like Sales Representative and Laboratory Technician. Employees with low job satisfaction and those in their first two years at the company are at the highest risk of leaving.
